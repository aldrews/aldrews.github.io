History

Past

Similar to all technologies, browsing started in an infant stage that could barely be recognized in today's standards as “browsing.” The real beginning can be traced back to the idea of what browsing may become. As stated in History of the Web, “An influential early exploration of how computers might revolutionize information is a 1945 essay by Vannevar Bush entitled “As We May Think”. This essay envisioned a machine called a memex that helps an individual human see and explore all the information in the world” (History of the Web). This shows how the concept of the “web” or even browsing was thought of before it could be created. Development for this did not actually begin until around 1988, when the first iteration of an online database was created. The History of the Web mentions, “Users can download a WAIS (Wide Area Information Servers) client to search through an array of distributed servers and access the documents stored there” (The History of the Web). With the creation of something the WAIS that can be shared and searched, it makes one of the first ideas of browsing come to life. After this idea is then passed from hand to hand and developed over the years, the first browser comes to life. This machine was the WorldWideWeb browser. Though it was advanced for its time, it did not gain much traction initially. The process takes many more years, along with several competitors arriving to start the first website. As The History of the Web says, “Tim Berners-Lee publishes the first website as a way of both demonstrating what the web was, and explaining its purpose” (The History of the Web). This step sped up the development process and made browsing take off. Developments such as the HTTP protocol, URLs, and the beginnings of Lynx came next to expand what browsing could accomplish. At this point, the browsing experience is still basic, but has the foundation to quickly develop and take over the stage of how the majority of people use the internet every day.

Present

The modern day usage and applications of browsing have changed just as much as the development of browsing. The original goal was to simply create and share information on a large scale. This has turned into a variety of utilities that do help share information, but also help in a plethora of different ways. As Surfing the Web explains, “Web browsers serve as our virtual passports to the internet, enabling us to access websites and explore the vast expanse of online content” (Surfing the Web). This gateway shows us and the introduction to a virtually infinite source of information. This information is a double edged sword, as the initial people sharing information were all pioneers sharing their research or opinions to further fields. Now the browsing experience is filled with clickbait or false information created to spread confusion or generate clicks for countless reasons. These reputable sources get hidden within the wave of gibberish and create an experience that is flooded with opinion and lies if the user is not careful enough to avoid them. Along with these new sources of browsing come the idea of websites. Instead of just sharing a list of documents that contain research or proofs, modern day websites can contain anything. From games, to blogs, to now AI websites that can do nearly any form of online work for the user. The technology is developing so rapidly that unless someone is truly invested in the culture, they will be left behind and engulfed in confusion upon returning. This future of browsing contains waves of advancements that are seen today such as mobile browsing, virtual reality shaping how we interact with browsing, and voice activated browsing showing how fast the fields can change. As Surfing the Web explains, “Whether you are a casual internet user or a tech enthusiast, understanding the intricacies of web browsers and the internet can empower you to make the most of this incredible tool” (Surfing the Web). This revolution in the online world that is constantly changing shows just how fast the present becomes the past and how people need to change just as fast to keep up with online topics such as browsing
